\begin{abstract}
A 3D city model is only as truthful as its building heights, and a model built from open public data usually gets
those heights not by measuring them but by inferring them, from a digital surface model, a floor count, or a
blanket prior, while presenting the result as if it were surveyed. Maqueta is a multi-modal geospatial fusion tool
that reconstructs real-world areas from open data (building footprints and roads from Overture, terrain from the
Copernicus digital surface model, land cover from ESA WorldCover, water and green from OpenStreetMap, population
from GHS-POP, and 2.5D building heights from Google Open Buildings), and its distinguishing choice is that every
building carries an explicit \emph{height provenance}: whether its height was measured, inferred from floors,
derived from the raster surface model, or assumed. This report reports that provenance across the 118 baked
places, and the result is a useful corrective to how open 3D city data is usually presented: only $8.8\%$ of
building heights are authoritative measurements, while $73.2\%$ are raster-derived, $11.6\%$ are blanket priors,
and $6.4\%$ are floor-count inferences, so more than nine in ten heights in an open-data city model are inferred,
not measured. Where authoritative LoD2 ground truth exists to check against, the fused heights carry a
building-height root-mean-square error of $3$ to $8$~metres with a downward bias, but such ground truth is itself
scarce: only two of the $118$ places have public LoD2 models to validate against. Making the provenance explicit
per building, rather than laundering inference into apparent measurement, is the report's contribution; every
number and figure regenerates from the committed benchmark.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{A} 3D city model is used for solar-potential studies, flood and wind simulation, visibility and
noise analysis, and urban planning~\cite{biljecki15}, and every one of those uses depends on the building heights
being right. Authoritative models at level-of-detail~2 (LoD2), reconstructed from airborne lidar, exist for a
handful of well-mapped countries~\cite{peters}, but for most of the world the heights are not surveyed; they are
\emph{inferred}, from a digital surface model, from a mapped floor count, or from a blanket assumption, and the
literature is clear that generating 3D city models without elevation data is a matter of estimation, not
measurement~\cite{biljecki17}. The problem in practice is not that heights are inferred, which is unavoidable at
global scale, but that the inference is usually hidden: a single-source extrusion is rendered as a crisp 3D city
and presented as if every height were known.

Maqueta takes the opposite stance. It is a multi-modal fusion tool that reconstructs an area from many open
sources, building footprints and roads from Overture Maps, terrain from the Copernicus GLO-30 surface model, land
cover from ESA WorldCover~\cite{worldcover}, water, green and rail from OpenStreetMap, population from GHS-POP, and
2.5D building heights from Google Open Buildings~\cite{sirko}, each layer carrying its source, licence and fetch
date, and it registers them onto the height map as the 3D scaffold. Its distinguishing choice is that every
building records \emph{how} its height was obtained, measured, floor-inferred, raster-derived or assumed, so the
model is honest about its own uncertainty. This report reads that provenance across the 118 baked places and
benchmarks the heights where authoritative ground truth exists. The contribution is not a new reconstruction
algorithm; it is the explicit, quantified provenance, and the corrective it provides to how open 3D city data is
usually presented.

\section{Height Provenance Across 118 Places}\label{sec:provenance}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.99\columnwidth]{fig-provenance.pdf}
\caption{The provenance of building heights across the 118 fused places. Only $8.8\%$ are authoritative
measurements; $73.2\%$ are derived from the raster digital surface model, $11.6\%$ are blanket priors, and
$6.4\%$ are inferred from a mapped floor count. More than nine in ten heights in an open-data city model are
inferred rather than measured, which the tool records per building rather than hiding.}
\label{fig:provenance}
\end{figure}

The core result is Fig.~\ref{fig:provenance}, and it is a plain statement of what an open-data 3D city model
actually is. Aggregated over the $118$ places and their millions of buildings, the height provenance breaks down
as: $8.8\%$ measured (an authoritative source gives the height directly), $6.4\%$ inferred from a mapped floor
count, $73.2\%$ derived from the Copernicus raster surface model (the difference between the surface and the
terrain, over the footprint), and $11.6\%$ a blanket prior where nothing better exists. The dominant source, by
far, is the raster surface model, which is an \emph{estimate} of height, not a measurement, and which is known to
carry metre-scale error and a systematic bias. The honest reading is that an open-data 3D city is overwhelmingly
inferred: fewer than one building in ten has a measured height. This is not a deficiency unique to Maqueta, it is
the reality of global open geodata, but Maqueta's choice to record the provenance per building means a downstream
user can filter, weight or distrust a height according to how it was obtained, rather than treating a raster
inference and a survey measurement as equally solid.

\section{Benchmark Where Ground Truth Exists}\label{sec:benchmark}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\columnwidth]{fig-benchmark.pdf}
\caption{Fused-height error against authoritative LoD2 ground truth, for the two of the $118$ places that have a
public LoD2 model (both in the Netherlands, from the 3D BAG). The root-mean-square error is $3.1$~m for Delft and
$8.3$~m for Amsterdam, both with a downward bias (heights under-estimated). That only two places can be validated
at all is itself the point: authoritative 3D ground truth is scarce, so the provenance record matters more.}
\label{fig:benchmark}
\end{figure}

Where an authoritative LoD2 model exists, the fused heights can be checked against it (Fig.~\ref{fig:benchmark}).
Two of the $118$ places have a public LoD2 reference, both in the Netherlands, whose national 3D BAG reconstructs
LoD2 models for all buildings from lidar~\cite{peters}. Against it, Maqueta's fused heights carry a
root-mean-square error of $3.1$~metres at Delft and $8.3$~metres at Amsterdam, with a mean absolute error of
$2.5$ and $7.5$~metres and a downward bias in both (the inferred heights systematically under-estimate the true
ones, by $0.1$~m at Delft and $6.6$~m at Amsterdam). The variation between the two, Delft accurate to a few
metres, Amsterdam off by more, tracks how much each relied on inference versus better sources, exactly the
distinction the provenance record captures. But the more important number is the denominator: only $2$ of $118$
places can be validated against authoritative ground truth at all, because LoD2 models are published for so few
places. That scarcity is why the provenance matters: for the other $116$ places there is no ground truth to
appeal to, and the honest thing a model can do is say, per building, how confident it should be.

\section{Discussion and Scope}\label{sec:discuss}

Maqueta's value is honesty about a fact the field usually elides: an open-data 3D city model is mostly inferred,
and the tool makes that inference explicit and auditable per building rather than rendering it as crisp truth.
The fusion itself is real and multi-modal, eight open layers registered onto a common height scaffold with full
source-and-licence provenance, and the benchmark, where it can run, quantifies the metre-scale height error. The
scope is stated plainly. The heights are inferred for over ninety percent of buildings, and the report says so;
the two LoD2 benchmarks are a thin validation set, not because the tool was tested lightly but because
authoritative 3D ground truth genuinely exists for almost nowhere, which is itself a finding. The reconstructed
scenes are for visualisation, teaching and planning context, not survey-grade models, and each carries an honest
height-provenance and source manifest so no layer is mistaken for more than it is. The reusable fusion core,
\texttt{geoscena}, turns any area of interest into a provenance-tracked scene bundle, and the product pipeline
bakes a curated registry of places on top of it. None of this is hidden; the interface shows the provenance of
every selected building.

\section{Conclusion}

Maqueta reconstructs 3D scenes of $118$ real places by fusing eight open geospatial modalities, and its honest
height provenance shows what such a model is made of: only $8.8\%$ of building heights are authoritative
measurements, the rest inferred from a raster surface model, a floor count or a prior. Where authoritative LoD2
ground truth exists, at just two of the $118$ places, the fused heights carry a $3$-to-$8$-metre error with a
downward bias. Recording that provenance per building, and reporting how scarce ground truth actually is, rather
than laundering inference into apparent measurement, is the report's discipline, and the reproducible fusion tool
that does it is its contribution.

\appendices
\section{Reproduction}\label{app:repro}

The committed benchmark (\texttt{data/derived/benchmark.json}) carries, per place, the layer counts, triangle
and size totals, the height-provenance mix (measured / floors / raster / prior) and, where a LoD2 reference
exists, the fused-versus-truth root-mean-square error, mean absolute error and bias. The figure scripts under
\texttt{manuscripts/geo-reconstruction/figures/} aggregate it into the global provenance mix and the LoD2
benchmark. The \texttt{geoscena} fusion core and the \texttt{maquetalab} pipeline are seeded and versioned, so a
place re-bakes deterministically; the open source layers are fetched under their respective licences and are not
re-hosted.
